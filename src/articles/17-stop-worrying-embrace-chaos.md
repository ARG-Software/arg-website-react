Fifteen years in this industry taught me one thing: we're exceptional at creating expensive problems disguised as innovative solutions.

And no, before you ask, I'm not talking about AI. Or rather, I'm not just talking about AI.

## The Bermuda Triangle of Development

You know that old saying? "Fast, cheap, good - pick two."

Well, in reality, most companies pick "fast and looks like it works" and pray nobody asks hard questions.

Money isn't flowing like it used to. And no, it's not AI's fault - it's because everyone finally realized we've been building card castles with JavaScript foundations and prayers. The market is risk-averse because, surprise, materialized risk hurts the wallet.

I work with clients who show up with systems completely blown up. The stack? A chaotic symphony of WordPress with Node, Node with Python, Python with C#, all orchestrated by the most unlikely maestro: the "cheap dev who only knew that one language."

The result? They pay for the same application 2, 3, sometimes 4 times.

## The Silver Bullet Seduction

Remember Web3? Those inflammatory posts saying that if you didn't learn blockchain, you should change careers?

And the Metaverse? Facebook bet so hard that they changed their name. Result: a handful of nothing and billions evaporated.

Now it's AI. And yes, it's powerful. But I've also rewritten AI-generated code several times. Detailed specifications, carefully constructed prompts… and still, spaghetti code that creates technical debt.

The inconvenient truth? These AI companies are drowning in investments they can't extract dividends from. The transition has to be gradual, otherwise you just accumulate resistance and frustration.

## The Paradox Nobody Wants to Admit

Here's the delicious irony: they use AI as an excuse to lower salaries and hire fewer juniors.

But if there are no juniors today, there won't be seniors tomorrow.

And without seniors, who's going to create the intellectual property to scrape and update the AI models?

Stack Overflow, once the Mecca of developers, is practically abandoned. Did you know AI drank tons of information from there? Yeah. We're literally killing the goose that laid the golden eggs while counting the eggs we still have.

## The Doomsday Cult of Tech

We have to learn to live with perpetual doomsday in our field. There's always a new apocalypse at the door, always a technology that will "destroy jobs" or "revolutionize everything."

Reality? Our field generates massive amounts of money. And there's always someone who wants to maintain the monopoly and pay workers as little as possible. AI is just the latest excuse.

Tech oligarchs position themselves as gods of the modern era, promising they'll "optimize" everything. Translation: replace you with something cheaper.

## So, What Now?

If you're in this field because you thought it would be smooth sailing, you need to revise your paradigm.

It's a well-paid field and will remain so. But it's extremely draining. We constantly change businesses, technologies, and paradigms. We have to put up with the delusions of billionaires who think they understand engineering because they hired engineers.

My advice? If you love it, study. Work on your technical skills, yes, but also on your personal ones. Networking, communication, negotiation - these are the skills that make the difference between a mediocre job and a great one, between a median salary and an excellent one.

Get interested in architecture and in concepts that transcend trendy language or frameworks. Write about what you learn (I do it on Medium, about architectures and technologies I use with clients).

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Tech isn't going to end. Jobs aren't all going to disappear.

But they will change. And they'll stay with those who adapt, with those who understand that technology is a tool, not a religion.

As long as there are companies wanting "fast and cheap" and discovering the hard way it was expensive, as long as there are legacy systems nobody understands but everyone depends on, as long as there's the next "revolution" needing to be integrated into the imperfect real world…

We'll have work.

Just don't expect it to be easy. It never was. And anyone who tells you otherwise is trying to sell you something.
